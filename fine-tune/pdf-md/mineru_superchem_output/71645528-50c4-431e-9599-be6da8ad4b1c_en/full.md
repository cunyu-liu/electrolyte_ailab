# Question

The chemical formula of salt A can be represented as  $\mathbf{M}(\mathbf{X}\mathbf{Y})_2$ , where the anion is highly toxic. The mass fraction of M in A is  $53.10\%$ , and both X and Y are second-period elements.

Dissolving A in a potassium salt solution with the same anion forms a red solution. Boiling it in air causes an oxidation reaction to produce a solution of yellow salt B. Reacting B with hydrochloric acid and diethyl ether yields colorless crystals C. C reacts with nitromethane in methanol to obtain D. Neglecting water of crystallization, the molar mass of D is  $42.08\mathrm{g / mol}$  greater than that of C.

Determine the correctness of the conclusions regarding the above content and select the option corresponding to all the correct conclusions.

Conclusion 1: The element  $\mathbf{M}$  can react directly with carbon monoxide to form a mononuclear carbonyl complex.

Conclusion 2: Theoretical prediction is that  $\mathbf{B}$  should be diamagnetic.

Conclusion 3: Compound  $\mathbf{D}$  contains two ligands in a 1:1 ratio.

Conclusion 4: There is only one type of atom coordinating to  $\mathbf{M}$  in compound D.

Conclusion 5: C exhibits a weak acidity comparable to that of hydrocyanic acid in aqueous solution.

A. All other options are incorrect  
B. Conclusion 1, Conclusion 2, Conclusion 3  
C. Conclusion 1, Conclusion 2, Conclusion 4  
D. Conclusion 1, Conclusion 2, Conclusion 5

E. Conclusion 1, Conclusion 3, Conclusion 4  
F. Conclusion 1, Conclusion 3, Conclusion 5  
G. Conclusion 1, Conclusion 4, Conclusion 5  
H. Conclusion 2, Conclusion 3, Conclusion 4  
I. Conclusion 2, Conclusion 3, Conclusion 5  
J. Conclusion 2, Conclusion 4, Conclusion 5  
K. Conclusion 3, Conclusion 4, Conclusion 5

# Answer

Correct Answer: H

# Detailed Explanation

The highly toxic ion composed of only second-period elements in a 1:1 ratio is  $CN^{-}$ , so we can write the equation:

$$
\frac {M (\mathbf {M})}{M (\mathbf {M}) + 2 \times (12.01 + 14.01)} = 53.10 \%
$$

Solving for  $M(\mathbf{M}) = 58.9(\mathrm{g / mol})$ , corresponding to cobalt or nickel. However, nickel cyanide complexes cannot be oxidized by air, which does not meet the requirements of the question. Therefore,  $\mathbf{M}$  is cobalt, and  $\mathbf{A}$  is  $Co(CN)_2$ .

# CHECKPOINT

2 PTS

$\mathbf{M}$  is cobalt, and  $\mathbf{A}$  is  $C o(C N)_{2}$

Cobalt reacts directly with carbon monoxide to only produce  $C o_{2}(C O)_{8}$ , which is not a mononuclear carbonyl complex. Conclusion 1 is incorrect.

# CHECKPOINT

1 PTS

Cobalt reacts directly with carbon monoxide to only produce  $C o_{2}(C O)_{8}$ , which is not a mononuclear carbonyl complex.

When  $Co(CN)_2$  is dissolved in potassium cyanide solution,  $Co$  is coordinated to form the deep red  $[Co(CN)_6]^{4-}$ . Boiling the solution in air,  $[Co(CN)_6]^{4-}$  is oxidized to the yellow  $[Co(CN)_6]^{3-}$ . Therefore,  $B$  is  $[Co(CN)_6]^{3-}$ .

# CHECKPOINT

1 PTS

B is  $[Co(CN)_6]^{3 - }$

$CN^{-}$  is a strong field ligand, and  $C o^{3+}$  has a high valence, resulting in a large splitting energy. Therefore,  $[C o(C N)_{6}]^{3-}$  is a low-spin complex, where  $C o$  has a  $d^{6}$  electron configuration, which in an octahedral field is a  $(t_{2g})^{6}(e_{g})^{0}$  electron configuration. All electrons are paired, so the complex is predicted to be diamagnetic. Conclusion 2 is correct.

# CHECKPOINT

1 PTS

$[C o(C N)_{6}]^{3 - }$  has a  $(t_{2g})^{6}(e_{g})^{0}$  electron configuration in an octahedral field, all electrons are paired, and it is diamagnetic

$\mathbf{B}$  is treated with hydrochloric acid and diethyl ether to obtain  $\mathbf{C}$ . Since complexes formed by  $+3$  valent cobalt have kinetic stability, it can be inferred that  $\mathbf{C}$  is likely the acid or acid hydrate corresponding to the anion, where the complex anion remains unchanged, still being  $[Co(CN)_6]^{3-}$ . Therefore, it is speculated that  $\mathbf{C}$  is

$H_{3}[C o(C N)_{6}]\cdot n H_{2}O$ , where  $n$  represents the number of crystal water molecules, which cannot be deduced from the existing conditions.

# CHECKPOINT

1 PTS

C is  $H_{3}[Co(CN)_{6}]\cdot nH_{2}O$

Ignoring crystal water, the molar mass of compound  $\mathbf{D}$  is  $42.08\mathrm{g / mol}$  more than that of  $\mathbf{C}$ , which corresponds precisely to 3 methylene groups. The possible reactions of nitromethane include oxidation and substitution reactions. Here,  $Co$  has already been oxidized to the stable  $+3$  valence, so a substitution reaction should have occurred. Note that the  $N$  atom in  $[Co(CN)_6]^{3-}$  has a lone pair of electrons and is nucleophilic. Therefore, it is speculated that the  $N$  atom undergoes an  $S_N2$  reaction by nucleophilic attack on nitromethane. Based on the molar mass, it can be deduced that  $\mathbf{D}$  is  $[Co(CN)_3(CNCH_3)_3]$ .

# CHECKPOINT

1 PTS

D is  $[C o(C N)_{3}(C N C H_{3})_{3}]$

D contains cyanide and methyl isocyanide ligands in a 1:1 ratio. Conclusion 3 is correct.

Since complexes formed by  $+3$  valent cobalt have kinetic stability, it can be inferred that the coordination of the  $C$  atom to  $Co$  will not change in the above nucleophilic substitution reaction. In  $\mathbf{D}$ , only the  $C$  atom is still coordinated to cobalt. Conclusion 4 is correct.

# CHECKPOINT

1 PTS

Only the  $C$  atom is still coordinated to cobalt in  $\mathbf{D}$ .

Since the basicity of the cyanide coordinated to cobalt is greatly reduced, its ability to bind to protons is reduced. Therefore, the acidity of  $\mathbf{C}$  is much stronger than that of hydrocyanic acid, and it is a strong acid in aqueous solution. Conclusion 5 is incorrect.

# CHECKPOINT

1 PTS

The acidity of  $\mathbf{C}$  is much stronger than that of hydrocyanic acid

In summary, choose option H.
