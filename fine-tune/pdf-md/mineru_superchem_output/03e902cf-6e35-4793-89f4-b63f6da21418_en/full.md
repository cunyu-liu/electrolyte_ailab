The second question is based on the Van't Hoff equation:

$$
\ln \frac {K _ {1} ^ {\mathrm {o}} (T)}{K _ {1} ^ {\mathrm {o}} (1 0 5 0 \mathrm {K})} = \frac {\Delta_ {r} H _ {m} ^ {\mathrm {o}} (1)}{R} \left(\frac {1}{1 0 5 0 \mathrm {K}} - \frac {1}{T}\right)
$$

$$
\ln \frac {K _ {2} ^ {\mathrm {o}} (T)}{K _ {2} ^ {\mathrm {o}} (1 0 5 0 \mathrm {K})} = \frac {\Delta_ {r} H _ {m} ^ {\mathrm {o}} (2)}{R} \left(\frac {1}{1 0 5 0 \mathrm {K}} - \frac {1}{T}\right)
$$

Subtracting the two equations and rearranging slightly, we have:

$$
\ln \frac {K _ {2} ^ {\mathrm {o}} (1 0 5 0 K)}{K _ {1} ^ {\mathrm {o}} (1 0 5 0 K)} - \ln \frac {K _ {2} ^ {\mathrm {o}} (T)}{K _ {1} ^ {\mathrm {o}} (T)} = \frac {\Delta_ {r} H _ {m} ^ {\mathrm {o}} (1) - \Delta_ {r} H _ {m} ^ {\mathrm {o}} (2)}{R} \left(\frac {1}{1 0 5 0 \mathrm {K}} - \frac {1}{T}\right)
$$

According to the question, at this temperature:

$$
p \left(\mathrm {S O} _ {2}\right) = 5 p \left(\mathrm {S O} _ {3}\right)
$$

In addition, under the initial conditions,  $p(\mathrm{SO}_2) = 2p(\mathrm{O}_2)$ . In reaction (2), the conversion rate of  $\mathrm{SO}_2$  and the conversion rate of  $\mathrm{O}_2$  are in a ratio of  $2:1$ , which is the same as the initial ratio. Therefore, the relationship  $p(\mathrm{SO}_2) = 2p(\mathrm{O}_2)$  always holds.

Then:

$$
\frac {K _ {2} ^ {\mathrm {o}} (T)}{K _ {1} ^ {\mathrm {o}} (T)} = \frac {p _ {\mathrm {S O _ {2}}} {} ^ {2} p _ {\mathrm {O _ {2}}}}{p _ {\mathrm {S O _ {3}}} {} ^ {2}} = \frac {5 ^ {2} \times \frac {5}{2}}{1} = 6 2. 5
$$

Substituting all known data, we have

$$
\ln {\frac {0 . 9 4 9}{0 . 1 2 0}} - \ln 6 2. 5 = \frac {2 7 \times 1 0 ^ {3}}{8 . 3 1 4} \left(\frac {1}{1 0 5 0} - \frac {1}{T}\right)
$$

At higher temperatures ( $>1400\mathrm{K}$ ), the mass of the solid will further decrease, producing a new reaction. At this time, the only gas produced by the decomposition reaction can be oxygen. The chemical reaction equation for this step is:

$$
4 \mathrm {C u O} \rightarrow 2 \mathrm {C u} _ {2} \mathrm {O} + \mathrm {O} _ {2}
$$

The new reaction occurring at this time is equivalent to adding extra oxygen to the original equilibrium. According to Le Chatelier's principle, the equilibrium of reaction (2) in the question shifts in the reverse direction, so  $p(\mathrm{O}_2)$  increases and  $p(\mathrm{SO}_2)$  decreases (these two substances are only related to the equilibrium of reaction (2)).

However, since the temperature remains constant, the equilibrium constant of the equilibrium remains unchanged, and  $p(\mathrm{SO}_3) = K_1^\circ p^\circ$ , so  $p(\mathrm{SO}_3)$  remains unchanged.
