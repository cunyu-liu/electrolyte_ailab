# Question

The main component of table salt is NaCl, and the chlorine content in table salt can be determined using the Mohr method. The operational steps are as follows:

(1) Accurately weigh  $1.3567\mathrm{g}$  of table salt, dissolve it in distilled water, and dilute to  $250\mathrm{mL}$  in a volumetric flask.  
(2) Transfer  $25.00 \mathrm{~mL}$  of this solution to an Erlenmeyer flask, add 6 drops of  $50.0 \mathrm{~g} / \mathrm{L} \mathrm{K}_{2} \mathrm{Cr}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{7}$  solution, and titrate with  $0.1000 \mathrm{~mol} \cdot \mathrm{L}^{-1} \mathrm{AgNO}_{3}$  solution to the endpoint, consuming  $22.61 \mathrm{~mL}$ . The blank experiment consumed  $0.05 \mathrm{~mL}$  of  $\mathrm{AgNO}_{3}$  solution.

Assume that the impurity ions in the table salt do not affect the reaction, and assume that  $\mathrm{Cr_2O_7^{2-}}$  is completely converted to  $\mathrm{CrO}_4^{2-}$ .

$$
K _ {\mathrm {s p}} (\mathrm {A g C l}) = 1. 8 \times 1 0 ^ {- 1 0}; K _ {\mathrm {s p}} (\mathrm {A g} _ {2} \mathrm {C r O} _ {4}) = 1. 2 \times 1 0 ^ {- 1 2}
$$

Then the following statement that is correct is:

A. All other options are incorrect  
B. The sodium chloride content in table salt is  $95.66\%$ .  
C. Assume the titration endpoint is exactly the stoichiometric point. The volume of each drop of  $50.0 \, \mathrm{g/L} \, \mathrm{K}_2\mathrm{Cr}_2\mathrm{O}_7$  solution is  $0.05 \, \mathrm{mL}$ , at which point  $\mathrm{Ag}_2\mathrm{CrO}_4$  precipitate forms.  
D. Under the condition of option C, the silver ion concentration in the solution is  $8.52 \times 10^{-6} \mathrm{~mol} \cdot \mathrm{L}^{-1}$  at this time.

E. To ensure that the endpoint error of this titration is 0, the volume of indicator solution to be added should be  $0.94 \mathrm{~mL}$ .  
F. The method has smaller errors and better results when titrating  $\mathrm{I}^{-}$ and  $\mathrm{SCN}^{-}$ .

# Answer

Correct Answer: E

# Detailed Explanation

Sodium chloride content calculation:

First, find the amount of  $\mathrm{AgNO}_3$  consumed by titration, and then obtain the amount of  $\mathrm{Cl}^-$ . Combined with solution dilution, extrapolate back to the amount of  $\mathrm{Cl}$  in the original salt sample, and then calculate the sodium chloride content. The calculation is as follows:

$$
\omega = \frac {0.1000 \times (22.61 - 0.05) \times 10 ^ {-3} \times (35.45 + 22.99) \times 10}{1.3567} = 97.18 \%
$$

Therefore, the sodium chloride content is  $97.18\%$ . B is incorrect

Assuming that there is only  $\mathrm{AgCl}$  precipitate and no  $\mathrm{Ag_2CrO_4}$  precipitate, then:

$$
\left[ \mathrm {A g} ^ {+} \right] = K _ {\mathrm {s p}} ^ {1 / 2} = \left(1. 8 \times 1 0 ^ {- 1 0}\right) ^ {1 / 2} = 1. 3 4 \times 1 0 ^ {- 5} (\mathrm {m o l} \cdot \mathrm {L} ^ {- 1})
$$

Assuming only one precipitate is formed, the calculated silver ion concentration is  $1.34 \times 10^{-5}(\mathrm{mol} \cdot \mathrm{L}^{-1})$ .

The original concentration of the indicator solution is approximately:

$$

The actual  $\mathrm{CrO}_4^{2-}$  concentration at the end of the titration is:

$$
\left[ \mathrm {C r O} _ {4} ^ {2 -} \right] = \frac {0 . 1 7 0 \times 2 \times 0 . 0 5 \times 6}{2 5 . 0 0 + 2 2 . 6 1 + 0 . 3 0} = 2. 1 \times 1 0 ^ {- 3} \left(\mathrm {m o l} \cdot \mathrm {L} ^ {- 1}\right)
$$

To generate  $\mathrm{Ag}_2\mathrm{CrO}_4$  precipitate, the concentration of  $\mathrm{CrO}_4^{2-}$  must be at least:

$$
\left[ \mathrm {C r O} _ {4} ^ {2 -} \right] = K _ {\mathrm {s p}} / \left[ \mathrm {A g} ^ {+} \right] ^ {2} = 6. 6 \times 1 0 ^ {- 3} \mathrm {m o l} \cdot \mathrm {L} ^ {- 1}
