# Question

Ammonium sulfide is a widely used qualitative analytical reagent. To prepare this reagent, hydrogen sulfide gas is introduced into  $4\sim 5 \mathrm{~mol} / \mathrm{L}$  aqueous ammonia, followed by the addition of water. Pure solutions cannot be obtained by this method: when absorbing gas below or above the stoichiometric amount, the solution will contain excess ammonia or ammonium hydrosulfide.

Dilute  $10.00~\mathrm{mL}$  of the ammonium sulfide test solution to  $1.000~\mathrm{L}$ , then transfer  $10.00~\mathrm{mL}$  of the resulting stock solution to a distillation flask and add approximately  $40~\mathrm{mL}$  of water. Next, add  $25.00~\mathrm{mL}$  of  $0.1~\mathrm{mol/L}$  cadmium nitrate solution to the collection flask (where the distilled components will condense), and introduce  $20.00~\mathrm{mL}$  of  $0.02498~\mathrm{mol/L}$  sulfuric acid into the distillation flask.

Distill approximately half of the solution from the distillation flask into the collection flask. (A yellow precipitate can be observed in the collection flask.)

Thoroughly wash the remaining contents in the distillation flask into a conical flask, add a few drops of methyl red indicator, and titrate with  $0.05002\mathrm{mol} / \mathrm{L}$  NaOH solution, consuming  $10.97~\mathrm{mL}$  of NaOH at the endpoint.

Add bromine water to the solution in the collection flask (the precipitate dissolves), heat and boil the solution for 15 minutes to remove excess bromine. The bromine oxidizes all sulfur-containing ions to sulfate ions. Titrate the solution in the collection flask with  $0.1012\mathrm{mol} / \mathrm{L}$  NaOH, consuming  $15.01~\mathrm{mL}$ .

# Answer

Correct Answer: I

# Detailed Explanation

Ions remaining in the distillation flask are  $\mathrm{NH}_4^+$  and  $\mathbf{H}^{+}$ , and only  $\mathbf{H}^{+}$  is consumed when methyl red is used as an indicator.
