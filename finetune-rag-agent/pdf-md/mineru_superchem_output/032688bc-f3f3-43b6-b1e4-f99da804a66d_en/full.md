# Question

$\mathbf{M}$  is a gray-white metallic element, soluble in acids and bases, and highly toxic. It exists in nature in the form of silicate minerals with a  $+2$  oxidation state.  $\mathbf{M}$  reacts with chlorine gas to yield a white chloride  $\mathbf{A}$ ;  $\mathbf{A}$  reacts with tetraphenylphosphonium chloride in a  $1:2$  molar ratio to yield  $\mathbf{B}$ , while reacting in a  $1:1$  molar ratio yields  $\mathbf{C}$ .  $\mathbf{B}$  and  $\mathbf{C}$  are both  $2:1$  type salts. When  $\mathbf{A}$  is dissolved in a mixture of dinitrogen tetroxide and ethyl acetate at low temperature,  $\mathbf{D}$  precipitates, containing only  $+1$  valent cations and  $-2$  valent anions, and no isolated solvent molecules; when  $\mathbf{D}$  is heated to  $50^{\circ}C$ , a reddish-brown gas  $\mathbf{E}$  is released, and  $\mathbf{F}$  is obtained; when  $\mathbf{F}$  is further heated, a mixture of gases  $\mathbf{E}$  and  $\mathbf{G}$  is released, and compound  $\mathbf{H}$  is obtained;  $\mathbf{H}$  is a tetranuclear complex with the same symmetry as a methane molecule.

A. All other options are incorrect.  
B. Metal  $\mathbf{M}$  is the metal with the weakest X-ray penetration ability.  
C. The cations and anions in B possess different symmetries.  
D. B and C have different coordination numbers for the metal M.  
E. D, F, and H contain exactly the same elements.  
F. H contains only one kind of bidentate ligand.  
G. E and G are diamagnetic at room temperature.

# Answer

Correct Answer: E

# Detailed Explanation

# 1. Identification of Metal M

$\mathbf{M}$  is a gray-white metallic element, soluble in both acids and bases (amphoteric), and highly toxic. It exists in nature in the  $+2$  oxidation state.

Among common amphoteric metals, Aluminum (Al) does not meet the oxidation state condition, Zinc (Zn) is not highly toxic, and Tin (Sn) exists in nature mostly in the  $+4$  oxidation state, which does not meet the common natural oxidation state condition. Lead (Pb) meets the natural oxidation state condition, but it mainly exists as the sulfide PbS. None of these meet the requirements of the question.

At this point, consider the diagonal relationship. Due to the  $+2$  valence state, consider Beryllium Be, the diagonal of Al, which is gray-white, amphoteric (soluble in acids and bases), highly toxic, and exists in nature in the  $+2$  oxidation state (such as in beryl).

Therefore,  $\mathbf{M}$  is beryllium (Be).

# 2. Identification of Compound A

$\mathrm{M}(\mathrm{Be})$  reacts with chlorine  $(\mathrm{Cl}_2)$  :  $\mathrm{Be} + \mathrm{Cl}_2 \rightarrow \mathrm{BeCl}_2$

A is a white chloride, consistent with the properties of  $\mathrm{BeCl}_2$  (white solid).

Therefore,  $\mathbf{A}$  is  $\mathrm{BeCl}_2$

3. Identification of Compounds B and C

A  $(\mathrm{BeCl}_2)$  reacts with tetraphenylphosphonium chloride  $\mathrm{(PPh_4Cl)}$

$\mathrm{PPh_4Cl}$  provides  $\mathrm{PPh_4}^+$  and  $\mathrm{Cl^-}$ ,  $\mathrm{BeCl_2}$  is a Lewis acid and can form chloro complexes.

A  $1:2$  molar ratio reaction yields B:

$$
\mathrm {B e C l} _ {2} + 2 \mathrm {P P h} _ {4} \mathrm {C l} \rightarrow (\mathrm {P P h} _ {4}) _ {2} [ \mathrm {B e C l} _ {4} ].
$$

The anion  $\left[\mathrm{BeCl}_4\right]^{2-}$  has a  $-2$  charge, and the cation  $\mathrm{PPh}_4^+$  has a  $+1$  charge, forming a  $2:1$  type salt (cation number: anion number  $= 2:1$ ).

Therefore,  $\mathbf{B}$  is  $(\mathrm{PPh}_4)_2[\mathrm{BeCl}_4]$ .

2 \mathrm {B e C l} _ {2} + 2 \mathrm {P P h} _ {4} \mathrm {C l} \rightarrow \left(\mathrm {P P h} _ {4}\right) _ {2} \left[ \mathrm {B e} _ {2} \mathrm {C l} _ {6} \right].

$\mathrm{BeCl}_2$  can dimerize in solution to form the  $[\mathrm{Be}_2\mathrm{Cl}_6]^{2-}$  anion (containing a chlorine-bridged structure).

The anion  $\left[\mathrm{Be}_2\mathrm{Cl}_6\right]^{2-}$  has a  $-2$  charge, and the cation  $\mathrm{PPh}_4^+$  has a  $+1$  charge, forming a  $2:1$  type salt (cation number: anion number  $= 2:1$ ).

Therefore,  $\mathbf{C}$  is  $(\mathrm{PPh}_4)_2[\mathrm{Be}_2\mathrm{Cl}_6]$ .

# 4. Identification of Compound D

A  $(\mathrm{BeCl}_2)$  is dissolved in a mixture of dinitrogen tetroxide  $\left(\mathrm{N}_2\mathrm{O}_4\right)$  and ethyl acetate, and D precipitates out at low temperature.

$\mathrm{N}_2\mathrm{O}_4$  dissociation:  $\mathrm{N}_2\mathrm{O}_4 \rightleftharpoons \mathrm{NO}^+ + \mathrm{NO}_3^-$ .

Ethyl acetate  $\left(\mathrm{CH}_{3} \mathrm{COOC}_{2} \mathrm{H}_{5}\right)$  may participate in the reaction or hydrolysis to provide acetate  $\left(\mathrm{CH}_{3} \mathrm{COO}^{-}\right)$ , but at low temperature, the main reaction is the

displacement of chloride by nitrate.

D contains only  $+1$  valent cations and  $-2$  valent anions, with no isolated solvent molecules.

The cation is  $\mathrm{NO}^+$  (from  $\mathrm{N}_2\mathrm{O}_4$ ), and the anion is a -2 valent anion containing Be.

$\mathrm{BeCl}_2$  reacts with  $\mathrm{NO}_3^-$  to form  $\left[\mathrm{Be}(\mathrm{NO}_3)_4\right]^{2-}$

Therefore,  $\mathbf{D}$  is  $(\mathrm{NO})_2[\mathrm{Be}(\mathrm{NO}_3)_4]$ , consistent with a  $2:1$  type salt.

5. Identification of Compounds E, F, G, and H

$\mathbf{D}$  is heated to  $50^{\circ}C$  to obtain  $\mathbf{E}$  and  $\mathbf{F}$ :

$(\mathrm{NO})_2[\mathrm{Be}(\mathrm{NO}_3)_4]$  decomposes upon heating, releasing the reddish-brown gas  $\mathrm{NO}_2$ .

Therefore,  $\mathbf{E}$  is  $\mathrm{NO}_2$ , and  $\mathbf{F}$  is  $\mathrm{Be}(\mathrm{NO}_3)_2$ .

Continued heating leads to the loss of  $\mathrm{NO}_2$ . Some of the nitrate ligands in the complex are converted to oxide ions. Furthermore, according to the question, this complex is similar to methane and has  $T_{d}$  symmetry. Therefore, the 4 Be atoms it contains are located at the vertices of a tetrahedron. From symmetry, the nitrate groups are distributed along the edges of the tetrahedron, with a total of 6. Valence calculation reveals that one  $\mathrm{O}^{2-}$  ion can only be located at the center, coordinated to the 4 Be atoms. In this case, each Be atom is in a tetrahedral coordination environment, which is more reasonable.

Therefore,  $\mathbf{H}$  is  $\mathrm{Be}_4\mathrm{O}(\mathrm{NO}_3)_6$
