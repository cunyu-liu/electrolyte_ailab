# Question

Chromium is a steel-gray metal extracted from red Siberian ore by the French scientist Vauquelin in 1797. It is often used in the manufacture of stainless steel and for chrome plating metal surfaces of instruments. Industrially, the preparation of elemental chromium starts from chromite  $(\mathrm{FeCr}_2\mathrm{O}_4)$ , which is strongly heated in air with a mixture of sodium carbonate, where iron is converted to ferric oxide (Reaction 1). The resulting solid residue is leached with water, acidified, and a chromium-containing sodium salt precipitates. This salt is then reduced to chromium oxide by heating with carbon at high temperature, simultaneously generating a salt and releasing a gas (Reaction 2). Finally, treatment with ignited metallic aluminum yields relatively pure chromium (Reaction 3).

For the extraction of molybdenum, which is in the same group as chromium, molybdenite is usually used as the raw material, which is roasted in oxygen (Reaction 4). The resulting product is leached with ammonia water to transfer molybdenum to the solution; alternatively, molybdenite can react with caustic soda solution in an oxygen atmosphere to transfer molybdenum to the solution (Reaction 5), during which sulfur is also oxidized and enters the solution. Subsequently, the ammonia-leached solution is acidified to precipitate a polyoxometalate which anion contains seven molybdenum atoms (Reaction 6, ionic equation), which is then calcined and reduced with hydrogen to obtain relatively pure metallic molybdenum.

4 \mathrm {F e C r} _ {2} \mathrm {O} _ {4} + 8 \mathrm {N a} _ {2} \mathrm {C O} _ {3} + 7 \mathrm {O} _ {2} \rightarrow 2 \mathrm {F e} _ {2} \mathrm {O} _ {3} + 8 \mathrm {N a} _ {2} \mathrm {C r O} _ {4} + 8 \mathrm {C O} _ {2}
