# Question

Bright green A ( $\omega_{\mathrm{Cu}} = 57.5\%$ ) and blue B ( $\omega_{\mathrm{Cu}} = 55.3\%$ ) are widely found in nature, and these two minerals often coexist. Heating a mixture of copper sulfate solution and sodium carbonate in a sealed tube to  $150 - 170^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$  yields A (Reaction 1), while a lower reaction temperature produces B (Reaction 2). ( $\omega\%$  represents mass fraction.)

Heating A or B within a certain temperature range decomposes them to produce copper oxide. Take  $7.96\mathrm{mg}$  of  $\mathrm{CuO}$  in a beaker, add  $100\mathrm{mL}$  of deionized water, and stir continuously while adding solid  $(\mathrm{NH}_4)_2\mathrm{CO}_3$ . Assuming the solution volume remains constant at  $100\mathrm{mL}$ , calculate the minimum mass of  $(\mathrm{NH}_4)_2\mathrm{CO}_3$  required to fully dissolve the solid (assuming no other basic salt precipitates form and no  $\mathrm{CO}_2$  escapes the system). Also, determine the chemical equations for Reaction 1 and Reaction 2.

Based on the mass fraction of  $\mathrm{Cu}$ , the chemical formulas of  $\mathbf{A}$  and  $\mathbf{B}$  can be deduced as  $\mathrm{Cu}_{2}(\mathrm{OH})_{2}\mathrm{CO}_{3}$  and  $\mathrm{Cu}_{3}(\mathrm{OH})_{2}(\mathrm{CO}_{3})_{2}$ , respectively.

Since  $\mathrm{CuO}$  is highly insoluble,  $(\mathrm{NH}_4)_2\mathrm{CO}_3$  is present in large excess compared to  $\mathrm{CuO}$ . Therefore, it can be assumed that the  $\mathsf{pH}$  of the solution after the solid dissolves is approximately equal to the  $\mathsf{pH}$  of a pure  $(\mathrm{NH}_4)_2\mathrm{CO}_3$  solution. For a pure  $(\mathrm{NH}_4)_2\mathrm{CO}_3$  solution, the charge balance equation is:

$$
\left[ \mathrm {N H} _ {4} ^ {+} \right] + \left[ \mathrm {H} ^ {+} \right] = \left[ \mathrm {O H} ^ {-} \right] + \left[ \mathrm {H C O} _ {3} ^ {-} \right] + 2 \left[ \mathrm {C O} _ {3} ^ {2 -} \right]
$$

Upon analysis, the analytical concentration of  $(\mathrm{NH}_4)_2\mathrm{CO}_3$  is greater than  $1.0 \times 10^{-3} \mathrm{~mol/L}$ , which is significantly higher than  $\mathrm{H}^+$  and  $\mathrm{OH}^-$ . Thus,  $[\mathrm{H}^+]$  and  $[\mathrm{OH}^-]$  in the above equation can be neglected, simplifying the charge balance equation to:

$$
\mathrm {[ N H _ {4} ^ {+} ] = [ H C O _ {3} ^ {-} ] + 2 [ C O _ {3} ^ {2 -} ]}
$$

Let the analytical concentration of  $(\mathrm{NH}_4)_2\mathrm{CO}_3$  be  $c$ . According to the distribution coefficient formulas:

$$
\left[ \mathrm {N H} _ {4} ^ {+} \right] = 2 c \cdot \frac {\left[ \mathrm {H} ^ {+} \right]}{\left[ \mathrm {H} ^ {+} \right] + K _ {a} \left(\mathrm {N H} _ {4} ^ {+}\right)}
$$

$$
\left[ \mathrm {H C O} _ {3} ^ {-} \right] = c \cdot \frac {K _ {a 1} \left[ \mathrm {H} ^ {+} \right]}{\left[ \mathrm {H} ^ {+} \right] ^ {2} + K _ {a 1} \left[ \mathrm {H} ^ {+} \right] + K _ {a 1} K _ {a 2}}
$$

$$
\left[ \mathrm {C O} _ {3} ^ {2 -} \right] = c \cdot \frac {K _ {a 1} K _ {a 2}}{\left[ \mathrm {H} ^ {+} \right] ^ {2} + K _ {a 1} \left[ \mathrm {H} ^ {+} \right] + K _ {a 1} K _ {a 2}}
$$

By solving these equations, the concentration of  $\mathrm{H^{+}}$  after the solid dissolves is calculated as  $[\mathrm{H^{+}}] = 6.4\times 10^{-10}\mathrm{mol / L}$ , and  $[\mathrm{OH^{-}}] = 1.6\times 10^{-5}\mathrm{mol / L}$ .
